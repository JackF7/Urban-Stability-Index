§ 1
Abstract

New York City has been strongly influenced by immigration for centuries, and the relationship between immigrant populations and urban stability has only deepened over time. This thesis explores this relationship through three different analytical frameworks: historical, economic, and mathematical. Through these analyses, the thesis argues that immigration is not merely a contributing factor to the demographic and economic stability of New York City, but that it is, in fact, the driving force.

The historical sections explore immigration's role in driving the industrialization of New York City from the 1840s through the twentieth century. The economic sections demonstrate that immigrants today make up 44.3 percent of the total labor force in New York City, pay over $103 billion in taxes annually to the metropolitan area, and are more likely to be at work and to be business owners than native-born citizens. The mathematical sections quantify this through the Urban Stability Index using census data. This index shows that immigration is simultaneously driving population growth, labor force vitality, and housing demand. It also shows that in order for New York City to simply remain at its current population levels, it requires at least 56,612 net international migrants annually, given its structural 91,239 annual domestic out-migration. The Trump administration's immigration policies are quantified as having set the city's Urban Stability Index at effectively zero, given that it has set immigration policy in place that will result in only approximately 66,000 net international migrants arriving in the coming year. This thesis concludes that immigration policy is, in the most literal arithmetical sense, the determining factor in New York City's demographic future.

§ 2
Introduction

New York City has never been a demographically self-sustaining metropolis. From the moment the city became a destination for mass migration in the nineteenth century, its population growth, its expansion of the labor force, and its economic development have been dependent upon the constant inflow of people from someplace else. This constant immigration is the key factor that has enabled New York City to become the most economically productive and the most resilient urban center in the United States. As immigration has been allowed to flow freely into the city, the city has grown. As federal policy has restricted immigration through the quota system of 1924, through the enforcement surges of the latter twentieth century, and now through the crackdowns of the second Trump administration, the city has shrunk or stagnated, only recovering when the flow of immigrants resumed.

This thesis considers the relationship between immigration and urban stability in New York City through three theoretical frameworks. The first two sections provide the historical and economic context that supports the thesis's central argument. Starting with the industrial immigration waves of the 1840s and continuing through the twentieth and twenty-first centuries, they demonstrate how each new generation of foreign-born New Yorkers has supplied the labor force, the entrepreneurial spirit, and the tax base that has sustained the city through periods of both economic boom and bust. The third section develops the thesis mathematically. Using primary source data supplied by the U.S. Census Bureau, the 2023 American Community Survey, and the most recent population analysis supplied by the New York City Department of City Planning,44 the thesis develops the Urban Stability Index — a composite statistic that measures the simultaneous contribution of immigration to New York's population growth, labor force vitality, and housing pressure, and that projects each of these variables forward under four different sets of federal immigration policy.

The numbers produced by the Urban Stability Index are not arbitrary. Rather, they are the mathematical manifestation of the pattern that has dominated New York City for nearly two centuries: the pattern in which the city grows when immigrants arrive and shrinks when they do not. Under the current enforcement policy of the Trump Administration, net international migration to New York City has fallen to just 66,000 per year. The central argument of this thesis is that this is no coincidence. It is a policy decision with quantified demographic effects that are already apparent.

§ 3
Immigration and the Making of Industrial New York

Since its founding, the United States, and particularly New York City, have been shaped by continuous inflows of immigrants who have provided the labor and cultural foundations of an emerging urban economy. The country's first significant wave of immigration began in the nineteenth century, around the 1840s. From that point until 1889, over 14 million immigrants entered the United States, with more than 70% of the new arrivals coming from Germany, Ireland, and the United Kingdom.1 During this period, immigration rates steadily increased. According to the U.S. Census Bureau, 1,713,251 people immigrated to the US between 1841 and 1850, while 5,246,613 arrived between 1881 and 1890.2 These migration trends — driven by a combination of economic, political, and social pressures abroad and the promise of opportunity and freedom in America — became the foundation for the country's industrial boom at the turn of the century.

Following the Civil War, during the Second Industrial Revolution, the United States transformed from a largely rural society into an urban, industrial economy. Immigrants were a driving force in this transformation. Immigration contributed substantially to nineteenth-century population growth amid declining fertility rates. By 1900, 85% of American industrial workers were immigrants or the children of immigrants.3 That a great industrialization of the country coincided with a new wave of immigration was no coincidence. The US population in 1880 was 50,155,783, with immigrants consisting of almost seven million of that number. Forty years later, in 1920, the population more than doubled, sitting at 105,710,620, with a similar increase in the total number of immigrants, totaling just below fourteen million.4

These numbers show a demonstrable impact on urban growth and the nation's labor force, yet still underestimate the full-scale contribution of immigration. The term "immigrant" refers to the first generation of people moving to a country. By including children and sequential generations, we can see the true impact of immigration in the US. In 1920, out of the 105 million Americans, 23 million were children of immigrants, meaning more than one-third of the population could be attributed to immigrants.5 Their combined contributions enabled the United States to meet the growing demands of mass production and the expansion of urban infrastructure. Early twentieth-century US immigration was, therefore, both a demographic phenomenon and a driving force behind the country's economic development.

During the American industrial era, people entered the country through various ports of entry, with the main access point in New York City at Ellis Island. Situated in New York Harbor near the Statue of Liberty, Ellis Island came to symbolize hope and opportunity for millions of immigrants — often called a "golden door" through which they could step into a new life in America — making New York City an enduring emblem and microcosm of the American immigrant experience. The island processed over twelve million immigrants and was the nation's largest and most active immigration station from 1892 to 1924.6 While immigrants arrived through various ports of entry, between 1900 and 1924, New York's Ellis Island welcomed over 84% of the country's new residents. Comparatively, the port with the next largest immigrant arrivals was Boston at just over 6% during the same period.7 Ellis Island thus stood at the heart of America's immigration system, playing a vital role during the Second Industrial Revolution as the gateway for the labor force that powered the nation's rapid industrialization and urbanization.

Major cities, like New York, emerged as centers of immigration during the twentieth century, shaping the economic growth and development of the United States. Census data from 1900 show that immigrants and their children made up a clear majority of the white population in many major US cities. In New York (76.6 percent), Chicago (77.3 percent), and Milwaukee (82.7 percent), foreign-born whites and their US-born children together comprised more than three-quarters of the population. These demographic patterns were the result of various economic and agricultural calamities in many parts of the world in the mid-to-late nineteenth century, prompting mass migration. A notable example of this trend was the peak of Irish emigration as a result of the Great Famine of 1845–1852, when nearly two million people emigrated to the United States.8 These patterns demonstrate what are commonly referred to as push and pull factors. Economic hardship, famine, and persecution pushed people from their homelands, while the promise of opportunity, freedom, and prosperity pulled them toward America's growing cities.

§ 4
New York in the Twentieth Century

In comparison to the rest of the country, New York City has always had a higher share of immigrant residents. At the beginning of the twentieth century, right before World War I, New York's foreign-born population stood at 40 percent.9 This number changed dramatically with the Immigration Act of 1924 (Johnson-Reed Act). The law set quotas on the number of immigrants that could enter the United States, allowing entry to just two percent of the total number of individuals of each nationality as recorded in the 1890 census. The law also completely prohibited entry to immigrants from Asia.10 Over the next fifty years, the number of immigrants to the United States dropped sharply. In 1970, just 18 percent of the population was foreign-born. Not until almost eighty years later did the city begin to see its immigrant population return to its previous levels.11 The rebound was in large part due to the Immigration Act of 1965, which abolished the national origins system and made it illegal to discriminate in visa issuance on the basis of race, sex, nationality, place of birth, or place of residence. Without the quotas, non-European immigration spiked. However, the act also introduced limits on immigration from Latin America, creating negative attitudes toward migration from Mexico.12 This shift in immigration policy unfolded against a backdrop of postwar American prosperity and global realignment.

The United States did not experience massive geographic and economic destruction from World War II, unlike many countries in Europe and Asia. In fact, the war famously brought the country out of the Great Depression and into a golden age of economic expansion. Thus, World War II and the subsequent Cold War became the impetus for much of twentieth-century American immigration. After World War II, the United States had an increasingly significant international media presence, transmitted via television, radio, magazines, newspapers, and other programs. The public messaging projected an image of America as a land of opportunity. With the passage of the Immigration Act of 1965, more and more people began seeing the US as a potential new home, and US refugee policies made it easier for certain groups to come to the country. New York, in particular, received many Soviet Jews and Cubans.13 By the end of the twentieth century, US immigration policy remained politically charged, with stricter enforcement and heated debates over who belongs. This marked a sharp contrast with the more open, quota-free beginnings of the century amid growing globalized migration.

New York City has long been a magnet for immigrants, but the factors driving migration to the city are ever changing. The New York of the nineteenth century differs greatly from the city today; beneath all its changing dynamics, however, its identity as a city of immigrants has endured. Over the twentieth and twenty-first centuries, New York's immigrant population has grown increasingly diverse, reflecting broader global migration patterns. Today, the city continues to attract immigrants from around the world, with especially strong migration from Latin America, alongside arrivals from Europe, Africa, and Asia.

§ 5
Economic Stabilization

There are currently more than three million immigrants who call New York home, accounting for almost 40% of the city's population. As of 2022, the top three countries of birth for foreign NYC residents are, in order, the Dominican Republic, China, and Mexico.14 Just as immigrants in the nineteenth and twentieth century powered industrial growth, today's immigrants make up a significant share of the workforce, sustaining the city's economy. In 2023, New York had nearly two million foreign-born workers over the age of sixteen. This population made up 44.3% of the city's total labor force, which was well over double the national average of 18.6%.15 Many of them "work in industries that pay less than the total private sector, such as health care and social assistance and accommodation and food services."16 Over a century after the major immigration waves of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, New York is still relying on immigrant labor. These immigrants do not work in expendable industries, either; health care and food services are what many would deem "essential" to a functioning society. Their work helps stabilize the city's economy and population in the face of shifting demographics.

In 2023, immigrants in New York State, about two-thirds of whom reside in New York City, were more likely to be employed than US-born citizens.17 Moreover, research from the Kellogg School of Management at Northwestern University found that immigrants are 80% more likely than US-born citizens to start a business in the United States, creating more jobs and improving economic outcomes.18 These findings challenge the common misconception that immigrants take jobs from US-born citizens. In reality, they help expand the labor market. Immigration has a multiplier effect on the economy; immigrants fill job roles while simultaneously creating them.

Beyond their labor contributions, immigrant New Yorkers fuel significant economic growth through one of the most fundamental civic duties: paying taxes. According to the American Immigration Council, immigrants in the New York Metro area paid $103.3 billion in taxes in 2023.19 It is important to note that immigrants make use of public services like education and health care, as most taxpayers do. However, these costs do not negate the benefits: "immigrants' economic contributions far outweigh the cost of additional public services they incur."20 New York is not the only city to see these effects. Four cities in upstate New York — Syracuse, Utica, Buffalo, and Rochester — have experienced a resurgence in population and economic growth due to immigration.21 Federally, it is estimated that a mere 10% reduction in asylum seekers to the United States per year would cause a $1.5 billion loss in tax revenue and $8.9 billion to the economy over five years.22

Immigration has a multiplier effect on the economy: immigrants fill job roles while simultaneously creating them.

The presence of immigrants in the country demonstrates notable economic benefits, and the absence of immigration also presents significant takeaways. Immigration has enabled economic growth despite the sharp deceleration in the growth of the US-born workforce. Over the past decade, the prime workforce in the United States — workers between the ages of 25 and 54 — would have seen fundamentally no growth and, at times, a decline, without immigration. In 2014, the US prime labor force would have experienced a 2.6% decrease in the number of people, but immigration offset this decline, contributing enough new workers to sustain growth at 6.8%.23

Despite their large presence, evidence shows that immigration does not take jobs away from US-born workers because the number of jobs in an economy is not fixed and grows with labor supply and demand. Immigrants support the economy both as workers, increasing potential output, and as consumers, boosting aggregate demand. Historical examples, such as the post-pandemic economic recovery, showed that rising immigration coincided with historically low unemployment rates for US-born workers, indicating that there is no inherent trade-off between immigrant inflows and domestic employment. Conversely, policies like mass deportation can undermine economic stability for all workers by reducing demand and disrupting industries that depend on immigrant labor.24

Fertility and Population Growth

The number of births and total fertility rate in the United States have been declining for decades. For a population to maintain long-term stability, the fertility rate must remain around 2.1 births per woman. This replacement level is slightly higher than 2.0 because not every person will survive to reproductive age, choose to have children, or be able to do so.25 In 1990, the fertility rate in the United States was around 2.1 births per woman; this has since decreased to 1.62 births per woman in 2023.26 As a result of the rapidly decreasing fertility rate, the United States could soon see a shrinking population, leading to slower economic growth and an aging population. While there are several potential strategies to address this crisis, such as pro-natalist policies,27 one of the most natural and already occurring solutions is immigration.

Immigration as a form of population stabilization is not a new concept. Around the world, an increasing number of countries have seen declines in their total fertility rates. However, these downturns have not immediately led to population loss. In numerous countries, including Italy, Germany, and Monaco, net immigration has reversed population loss this century. In Italy, for instance, where the fertility rate is only 1.3 births per woman, the population increased by 2,689,200 people between 2000 and 2020; without immigration, the nation's population would have decreased by 1,576,100. In other countries, such as Japan and Greece, population decline has been less severe largely due to immigration.28 Through immigration and with pro-immigration policies, nations can counteract population decline and maintain stable demographic growth.

Although immigration acts as a stabilizing force for population growth, it is only a partial remedy. As native fertility rates decline, countries will require ever-increasing levels of immigration to counteract the trend, making it insufficient as a standalone solution. In 2025, the fertility rate for foreign-born women in the United States is 1.88 births per woman; this is slightly higher than the total fertility rate of 1.62, but lower than the replacement level of 2.1. While immigration alone cannot fully reverse long-term demographic decline, it remains the most reliable force offsetting it. Due to falling birth rates, all population growth in the United States could be attributed to immigration between 2022 and 2023.29 In this context, a city like New York, where immigration remains high and foreign-born communities are deeply embedded in the labor force, demonstrates how migration can sustain both population stability and economic vitality, even in the face of national demographic decline.

§ 7
Policy, Deportations, and Migration

Immigration flows do not occur in a political vacuum. Federal law determines how many people choose to, or are allowed to, immigrate to the United States in any given year. Immigration policy in the United States cannot be reduced to a simple liberal–conservative divide. Since the late 1980s, both major political parties have largely supported restrictive immigration frameworks, even as they have differed in rhetoric and emphasis. The Immigration Reform and Control Act (IRCA) of 1986 was a bipartisan compromise that paired amnesty with expanded enforcement, most notably through employer sanctions designed to deter future unauthorized migration.30 In the decades that followed, Congress moved further toward enforcement-first approaches, exemplified by the Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act of 1996, enacted under a Democratic president, which significantly expanded deportation authority and border control.31 More recent efforts at relief have followed a similar pattern: the Obama administration's creation of Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals (DACA) was accompanied by intensified border enforcement and record numbers of removals. Government policy plays a central role in determining whether immigration functions as an engine of stability or a source of demographic volatility.

During President George W. Bush's first term, the September 11 attacks in 2001 were a turning point for the country, leading to an increase in anti-Muslim hate crimes.32 Following the attacks, President Bush told former CBP commissioner Rob Bonner, "You've got to secure our borders against a terrorist threat, Bonner. But you have to do it without shutting down the U.S. economy."33 Anti-immigrant sentiments throughout the country became excuses for stricter immigration policies and discriminatory measures. After 9/11, the number of tourist and business traveler visas fell sharply, declining by 2.6 million, or 44%, by 2003.34

One of the Bush Administration's most significant changes was the creation of the Department of Homeland Security and with it the Bureau of Immigration and Customs Enforcement, now known as ICE.35 Twenty-three years later, on July 4, 2025, after the passage of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, ICE became the largest, most well-funded federal law enforcement agency in US history.36 In 2025 alone, under Donald Trump's second presidential administration, the agency has refused to provide due process to people it has detained and arrested legal immigrants exercising their freedom of speech. Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem set a minimum daily quota of 3,000 ICE arrests, which has resulted in workplace raids and racial profiling.

In both of his presidential terms, Donald Trump has used his federal authority to significantly reduce the number of immigrants coming into the country. During the first few months of his first term, Trump and his administration implemented a series of travel bans that restricted entry into the United States and halted refugee processing for individuals from several countries, including Iran, Somalia, Sudan, and Venezuela.37 Over the next four years, the Trump Administration enacted 472 executive actions regarding immigration policy.38 Beyond direct policy effects, Donald Trump has also contributed to a broader climate of anti-immigrant sentiment by normalizing exclusionary rhetoric. According to a 2023 Siena College poll, 82% of New York State voters believe "the recent influx of migrants to the state is a serious problem, with 54% saying very serious."39

Democratic and Republican presidents alike have used their powers to restrict immigration access and deport people from the country. President Barack Obama's administration deported over 3.1 million people during his eight years in office. In comparison, the first Trump Administration deported 932,000 people.40 Unlike Trump, however, Obama attempted to provide more legal pathways for immigrants, most notably through DACA in 2012, which provided temporary protection from deportation to some undocumented immigrants who came to the country as children.41

In January 2024, the number of "likely entries" at the border stood at roughly 124,000. A year later, in February 2025, that number was zero.

In January 2024, under President Joe Biden, the number of "likely entries" — those who receive either a parole status or notice to appear in an immigration court — stood at roughly 124,000. A year later, in February 2025, that number was 0.42 Before the clock struck 12:01 PM on Trump's inauguration day, US Customs and Border Protection announced the end of the Biden-era CBP One app, which allowed people to schedule immigration appointments at eight southwest border points of entry.43 As a result, attempts to immigrate to the US have dropped sharply. In fiscal year 2025, the US-Mexico border recorded just 444,000 migrant encounters, whereas the year before saw 2.1 million.44 The consequences of these actions extend beyond migrants themselves. Changes in immigration flows affect the broader population, with significant implications for economic performance, labor markets, and long-term population growth.

§ 8
Mathematical Analysis and Stability

We can translate historical and current demographic data and patterns into formal mathematical models to analyze how immigration has played various stabilizing roles with regard to New York's population, labor force, and housing. The preceding sections of this thesis have established that immigration has been a stabilizing force for New York City for multiple centuries. This section formalizes that argument mathematically.

To visualize the effect immigration has on New York both economically and demographically, I have created the Urban Stability Index (USI) — a system of differential equations and a composite index — to quantify immigration's contributions to population, labor, and housing stability in the city. The model draws on three core equations: a population equation derived from census and migration statistics, a labor force equation that measures the effects of new workers, and a housing pressure equation that imposes a penalty on the USI when population growth outpaces housing supply. The three equations are then combined into a single number, the USI, which allows us to interpret the overall stability of New York based on changing factors.52

Immigration was not simply a contributing factor, but rather the primary driver of population growth in New York toward the end of the twentieth century.

The Housing Model

The final part of the USI model revolves around housing demand. More immigrants means more workers, but it also requires the city to find homes for the new population. The following equation represents housing pressure by measuring the ratio of residents to occupied housing units.48

$$H(t) = \frac{P_i(t) + P_n(t)}{U(t)} \qquad H(2023) \approx 2.2$$
U grows at +20,000 new units/yr (NYC Construction Outlook Report historical average).49 Rising H(t) = more people per unit = penalty term.
The Urban Stability Index

To calculate the Urban Stability Index, we combine the three components into a single composite measure. The weights each reflect immigration's measured share of that dimension in New York City, normalized so the weights sum to 1.0. In 2023–2024, international immigration accounted for 144,098 out of 178,725 total positive population inputs (80.6%). Foreign-born workers constitute 44.3% of the labor force. Harvard's Joint Center for Housing Studies finds that immigrant households account for 25% of household growth nationally.5051

Weight derivation: 80.6 + 44.3 + 25.0 = 149.9
$$w_P = \frac{80.6}{149.9} \approx 0.53 \qquad w_L = \frac{44.3}{149.9} \approx 0.30 \qquad w_H = \frac{25.0}{149.9} \approx 0.17$$
The Urban Stability Index · scaled ×10
$$\text{USI}(t_0, t_f) = \underbrace{5.3}_{w_P} \cdot S_P \;+\; \underbrace{3.0}_{w_L} \cdot S_L \;-\; \underbrace{1.7}_{w_H} \cdot S_H$$
USI > 0 = growth · USI = 0 = stasis · USI < 0 = decline

§ 10
Conclusion

New York City's history has been defined by the same fundamental force for nearly two centuries: growth fueled by immigration, and contraction in the absence of immigration. This thesis has explored this force from a historical, economic, and mathematical perspective, and in all cases, the evidence leads to the same conclusion. The historical evidence reveals that all of the city's great periods of growth — from the industrialization of the nineteenth century to the urban renaissance of the late twentieth — have occurred in tandem with immigration. The economic evidence reveals that the city's labor force, tax base, and entrepreneurial economy today are all fueled by a foreign-born population that constitutes 44.3 percent of the city's workforce and injects more than $103 billion into the metropolitan economy each year. The Urban Stability Index simply confirms what history and economics imply. Immigration is the primary force shaping New York's demographic future.

The math is clear on that point. In 2023, NYC lost 91,239 citizens due to domestic out-migration, which is not something that any immigration policy can hope to influence. Against that loss, NYC's natural increase of 34,627 in 2023 is only partial compensation. The rest must be made up by international immigration, which must have been at least 56,612 in order for NYC's population not to decline. Under the Trump immigration policy, that number is currently around 66,000, which is just above the minimum but still falling. The Urban Stability Index calculates that NYC's immigration levels have created a USI of effectively zero, meaning that NYC is not gaining or losing population but balancing on the exact mathematical point of demographic stability. If immigration drops to 20,000 per year, the "severe restriction" scenario, then NYC's USI drops to −0.40, translating into a loss of almost 370,000 residents over ten years, along with a labor pool reduced by more than 440,000.

These are the measurable outcomes of particular policy decisions, framed in the same language that has always been used to describe the relationship between New York and immigration. The Johnson-Reed Act of 1924 reduced immigration and the city plateaued for decades. The Immigration Act of 1965 opened the doors again and the city revived. This is another moment in the history of that relationship, and the USI renders its significance visible in a way that rhetoric cannot. We must not view immigration policy as a debate about culture and identity that incidentally has some demographic effects. It is a direct intervention in the demographics of the city. The city that has always been defined by the arrival of people from elsewhere is now being defined, with equal precision, by their absence.
